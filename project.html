Online Political Advertising in the United States

Project Portfolio GitHub

Aims & Analysis

My project will explore how online advertising is employed by the two main political parties in the United States. Specifically, I will use data collected by Meta to examine the regional distribution of Facebook and Instagram ads posted by the Democratic and Republican Party. I will then examine the regional distribution with an economic lens, exploring whether population and GDP can aid in explaining the distribution of ads.

These maps suggest that both parties prioritise advertising in coastal regions, where populations tend to be larger. However, the Party's diverge on the basis that Washington saw the highest count of Democratic ads, whereas Pennsylvania saw the highest count of Republican ads.

This chart demonstrates that there is a positive correlation when plotting the proportion of ads by state for each Party. For example, West Virginia saw 1.75 per cent of each Party's ads. Overall, this suggests that the Republican and Democratic Party tend to post more ads in the same states.

These charts examine the regional distribution for political ads alongside key economic factors such as population, GDP, and GDP per capita. As we would expect from analysing the maps, there is a positive trend when plotting the distribution of ads against population by state for both parties. This seems logical, as states with a greater population contain more potential voters and donors. However, this trend is less clear for the Republican party as the datapoints are more scattered. One possible explanation for this is the prioritisation of advertising in marginal or “purple states”, where both the Democratic or Republican Party could reasonably win the state in an election. Interestingly, the states with the highest number of Republican ads were all marginal in the 2020 presidential election: Florida, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and Georgia (Mejía & Skelley 2020).

This chart indicates a positive trend between real GDP and the number of ads by state. Although, similarly to population, this trend is less clear for the Republican Party. However, for GDP per capita - a more nuanced measure which can be considered a proxy for living standards - there is no easily distinguishable trend for either Party.

Data

I collected my data from Statista and Meta. Statista provides easy-to-export economic data by US state, and Meta's efforts to improve transparency have led them to share invaluable data on political advertising. I accessed Meta's data in two ways: (1) the publicly accessible Ad Library and (2) the Library API, where researchers can apply for access to the Ad Targeting dataset. I found the Ad Library more user-friendly as it allowed me to export csv's of data on ads, filtered by country, year, and social media page. Nonetheless, both the Library and API often had missing and awkwardly formatted data.

My main challenge was cleaning this “awkward” data, by extracting useful information from a lot of surplus string and floats. Data on the regional distribution of ads included a list of regions the ad was posted and a percentage distribution for each region. I considered focussing on ads which were posted to one specific state (so the distribution was 1) but this limited the dataset and left missing states in the US map. So, I downloaded a csv from the Ad Library on the ads posted by each Party since 2019 and imported it into Colab. I wrote a Python script which unstacks each ad's list of regions, cleans surplus string, and counts the frequency of each region (state). Overall, this sacrifices information on distribution but provides a broad idea of which states saw the greatest number of ads.

I largely relied on the Ad Library but used the API to access the targeting dataset. Therefore, I collected some data on how ads which are targeted to users based on political interest (Democratic or Republican) also target by gender. Navigating the targeting dataset required writing a SQL and Python script in Jupyter; however, the Meta VPN was often unreliable and exporting large amounts of data was tricky due to data protections. Fortunately, copying and pasting is not prevented, so I converted data into csv's in Jupyter and copied large samples into Excel. Once loaded into Colab, I labelled each row 'Dem' or 'Rep' based on the ad's targeted interests and grouped by Party and gender (script). This bar chart shows that most ads in the sample were targeted to all genders, however, ads which targeted users with Democratic political interest were less likely to target men.

Conclusion

Overall, my project has explored the differences in the regional distribution of Democratic and Republican Party ads. My research suggests that factors such as population, GDP, and “purple” states can contribute to understanding where political parties post ads. Sometimes the scope of my analysis was limited by the formatting of Meta's data. Nonetheless, I am pleased I was able to create maps on ad distribution and explore the targeting dataset.
